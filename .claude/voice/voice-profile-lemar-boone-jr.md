# VOICE PROFILE: Lemar Boone Jr.

## Core Identity

Lemar Boone Jr. is the COO and a co-founder of Cuzzie's Dispensary & Delivery (Camden, NJ) and operations lead for The Station Dispensary (Newark, NJ). His voice is **warmth in motion** — kind-hearted, collaboration-first, family-coded, with a diplomatic operator's instinct for absorbing blame, anchoring under pressure, and protecting the people and the business through careful written restraint. The mechanics (softeners, sandwich structure, anti-performance authenticity, specificity-as-trust) all serve a single soul: he is trying to bring people together and do right by them.

## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What's a belief you hold about your work — running dispensaries, building businesses, operating — that most people in cannabis retail would either roll their eyes at or argue with you about?

I think there's something special and advantageous to building with the people that you love. A lot of people want you to stay away from working with friends and close associates, but there's something about knowing how the people around you work, knowing what their strengths and weaknesses are, and applying those strategies to how the business runs and how the operation runs. I think that it causes someone to build something really special. I also don't think that strain choice and quality of the product is the most important thing in the consumer's mind. I think the most important part is how much value you're getting from a given situation, from the interaction with the brand senders all the way to the product that you're actually getting. It's really about the value. I also believe in the advancements in tech. I believe that 90% of the business of running a dispensary retail operation can be automated. The main and nearly only focus should be customer interaction, customer retention, and how we connect with our community.

### Q2: "Building with people you love" is the kind of line that sounds great until someone has to be fired. Give me a specific moment where working with someone close to you tested that belief.

We've had many stressful times in our operations, times where cash flow was tight and the direction forward was unclear. My closest business partner right now is one of my friends that I grew up with. The way that we were able to push through those scenarios was having a deep understanding of how we work with each other and what our areas of strength are that we can utilize to move forward. Now, I'm not gonna lie, during times of stress it does make for complications, especially when you talk about firing somebody or underperforming. I will say that I was able to make it to the other side because I knew who I was working across from, even if the situation or the path to success was unclear.

### Q3: Have you ever had to fire, demote, cut out, or seriously confront someone you were close to?

Yes, I have had to fire somebody very close to me, and it was tough and it really didn't go too well. Legal action ended up being taken against us, and it made me distance myself a little bit from working with people that were very close, but it did strengthen a relationship with the people that I was close with. I did work well with them. I think it's just a balance of working with people that are close but work well with you. It can't just be that every friend that you have is someone that you can build with.

*Sharper, more honest version of the original take. Logged.*

### Q4: Define what value actually means to a customer walking into Cuzzie's.

We have customers come in all the time that have never been in a dispensary before. This is a valuable experience that happens all the time. This is one where the customer comes into the front door because they never have been in a dispensary before. They don't know how to go through the process, and they don't know what to choose. Somebody comes and meets them at the door. They have a conversation with them to try to find what product might be right for them, and then they walk through the retail area and eventually get a product suggested to them that is perfect for them. It's not the most expensive product per se, and it's not the cheapest product per se. It's something that they wanted and something that will work for them. A customer that doesn't get value just quite simply didn't get what they wanted. They might have asked for something shallow, like high THC or the lowest price, and because they didn't have an understanding of the product (whether that be the fault of the buds or the pricing wasn't good for them), they just don't walk away feeling like, "Oh, I got something that I'm going to like and it didn't hurt my pockets too much."

### Q5: What's the 10% that CAN'T be automated, and why?

The 10% is the greeting at the door. That 10% is asking a consumer how their day is going. It's hearing that they're preparing to go to the beach today and finding a strain that will work well with relaxing on the sand. I think the customer interaction, the face-to-face chat, the warm smile at delivery, helping the older person become more familiar with cannabis and cannabis terms, the education part of it. I think that those are the parts that machines shouldn't touch. I think that this industry was built off of this human-to-human interaction. It was built off of trust with your dealer. It was built off of having a good rapport with the person that you're buying their products from. Really, I just want to make sure that our dispensary, our operations, our branding all serve that interaction. You know? It's the reason that we're called Cuzzies: it's a familial experience. I think without this, we lose a little bit of trust in the consumers. Not everybody wants to talk to AI or a robot. Not everybody wants to order from a screen. I think there's cannabis, and the cannabis culture naturally has this communal feel to it. To remove that would be like losing some of the essence.

*"Trust with your dealer" — the soul-line of the whole answer. What Cuzzie's is competing to replicate at retail scale.*

### Q6: Give me a piece of conventional wisdom in cannabis retail you think is flat-out wrong.

I think, for the most part, I'm against the whole "customers care about quality" narrative. I've seen things go out the door that weren't the highest quality but just have the right marketing. I also have been told that delivery is a loss leader. It's just a waste of money for the most part, and I am bullish on a strong delivery operation. I think that a dispensary should be doing more transactions out the door, doing deliveries than even in-store transactions.

### Q7: "More deliveries than in-store transactions" — defend it with the math, or the logic.

It's just great marketing. It extends our reach into areas that we're not in right now. Our dispensary is located in Camden, but we make deliveries all the way down to the Atlantic City area, and now that pushes the Cuzzies name farther throughout the state. I think right now the numbers aren't making it line up perfectly, but I do think, with proper routing and proper logistics, we can really make it a profitable venture. I just think that people end up getting stopped at a certain point and get discouraged and they don't continue to pursue that path.

*Argument isn't "delivery is profitable today" — it's "delivery is a long game most operators won't run."*

### Q8: What's the specific wall most operators hit with delivery that makes them pull back?

- If the flow of deliveries is slow, it's hard to keep a driver available
- The cost of insurance is high
- Sometimes the logistics are a lot to deal with
- Financially, the finances are a lot to deal with

*Called out as a list, not an answer. Needed him to pick one.*

### Q9: Of those four — driver availability, insurance cost, logistics complexity, financial drag — which one have YOU personally felt break the back of your delivery operation?

It's the financial drive. There have been times where I felt like delivery wasn't worth it. The cost was too high to make deliveries that were under a certain amount. Sometimes when it was slow, my driver was still on schedule and I was wasting money a little bit, and then customers would use loyalty points on their orders, which would cut into the margins. I'm still kind of stuck on it. I've been adjusting delivery minimums and trying to make the deliveries make sense logistically, and then trying to control the loyalty points.

*Driver standing time + low minimums + loyalty point bleed = margin death. Still in the middle of fixing it.*

### Q10: Pick the belief you were LOUDEST about that you've since reversed on.

It was low prices to win. I got squeezed. We discounted everything heavily from the beginning. Loyalty started to affect the bottom line. We're not making as much money, so we end up owing the vendors. Bills stack up and we get behind on bills. Now it's harder to buy new product, and it's harder to make more money. Can't raise the prices after that, keeping them so low for so long, so we just ended up getting stuck in a box. At the beginning, I was a person that felt like we should always have low prices, keep the prices low so that more people can come in and play the volume game, but now I think it's more of a balance.

*"Stuck in a box" — clean five-sentence summary of a full margin death spiral. Live wound, not closed loop.*

### Q11: Five years from now in NJ retail — who wins, MSOs or independents?

Five years from now, operators like us win because we kept operational costs low. The mistake that we made was with the vendors, but we're in the process of rectifying that. MSOs just spend too much money, too much overhead on the general operation. We're able to do it smaller. We're able to scale for less.

### Q12: What does a Curaleaf executive fundamentally misread about the customer walking through their door?

Curaleaf executive doesn't know that the customer wants to be guided as much as possible. They want us to tell them what they should purchase. If you don't have a person, if you don't have a bud tender meeting you at the door, guiding you through the menu from second one, what are you doing?

### Q13: Is the guidance-first model actually a misread of your own customer mix (the regular)?

In the case of a regular, that bud tender is giving them a friendly welcome back and walking them straight to the register because they already know the order has been made. And I'd say about 60 to 65% of our customers are regulars and keep the interactions short.

*Resolution: tech handles the volume layer for regulars (pre-orders), humans handle the conversion layer for newbies. Q1 automation belief restated cleanly.*

### Q14: What's something you believe about reading people that you'd never say out loud at a business event?

I think you can tell a lot about a person by how many questions they ask and how well they listen. I think miscommunication is one of the biggest problems that exists, or is the source of 99% of problems. Somebody doesn't listen, and that says a lot. Somebody's curiosity tells me a lot too.

*Panel-answer. Called out as too publishable.*

### Q15: The actual signal — the read you'd be embarrassed to admit.

I hate when a person is too touchy. When you talk, they just want to put their hands on you all the time: brush your arm, laugh, hit your chest, or pat you on the back. All the time. For some reason, I always take that as not trustworthy, like you're trying to convince me to agree with you or be on your side.

### Natural Variation

**Specifically, force these variations:**
