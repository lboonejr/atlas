## Update 2026-07-10 (14:30 ET) — Option D picked: adjournment-request mechanics (not legal advice)

Lemar reacted checkmark on Option D in #decisions. Researched the procedural mechanics
only — this is NOT legal advice and nothing has been filed:

- **Governing rule:** NJ Directive #06-04, "Statewide Adjournment Procedure for Civil
  Trials and Arbitrations." All adjournment requests go to the **clerk's office**, not a
  freestanding motion filed with the judge in the first instance.
- **Timing:** requests must be made as soon as the need is known, and — absent good
  cause for the delay — **no less than 5 days before** the scheduled trial date. Trial
  is July 30, so the request would need to go in by roughly July 25 at the latest to be
  timely without a good-cause showing.
- **Sequence:** the requesting party must **first notify the adversary** (here, Rivkin
  Radler / Jason Gil) that a request is going to be made, THEN notify the clerk of the
  adversary's response. In other words, the clerk expects to hear both "I'm requesting
  an adjournment" and "here's what the other side said" — it isn't a unilateral,
  ex-parte ask.
- **Practical read:** since Rivkin Radler has already stated a position (consent
  conditioned on the $20K payment), that conditioned response IS "the adversary's
  response" — reporting it to the clerk as-is (rather than silence) is the honest
  version of step 2. A judge could still grant a short adjournment over an objection,
  but a self-filed request this close to trial with an opposed adjournment carries real
  risk without counsel, consistent with the earlier LSNJ-thread flag.
- **Self-represented note:** self-represented litigants get no special treatment or
  procedural help from the court and are held to the same Rules of Court as attorneys.
