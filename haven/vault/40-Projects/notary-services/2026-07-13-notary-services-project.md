## Update — 2026-07-14 08:20 ET — NJ requirements researched (Lemar asked in #decisions 07:44 ET and in #atlas thread "what does it take to become a notary")

**NJ Notary Public (standard commission) path:**
- Eligibility: 18+, NJ resident (or resident of a bordering state regularly employed in
  NJ); no conviction for a crime involving dishonesty or a 1st/2nd degree crime.
- Apply via the NJ Division of Revenue and Enterprise Services (DORES): $25 filing fee
  (+$5 convenience fee if filing online); the application must be endorsed by a State
  Legislator before submission.
- Non-attorney applicants must complete a 6-hour approved notary education course and
  pass an exam (renewal: 3-hour continuing-ed course + updated exam).
- After DORES processes the application, take the Commission Packet to the County
  Clerk's office within 90 days to swear the Notary Public Oath of Office.
- Commission term: 5 years, renewable in 5-year increments.
- Needs a notary seal/stamp (name, "Notary Public, State of New Jersey," commission
  expiration) and a journal (paper or electronic) to log notarial acts.
- NJ does not itself require fingerprinting/background checks for the commission.

**NJ Notary Signing Agent (NSA / loan-signing) path:**
- New Jersey does NOT recognize "Loan Signing Agent" as a separate state license — it's
  just an NJ Notary Public with additional loan-signing training/experience; authority
  comes entirely from the underlying notary commission plus NJ's Revised Uniform Law on
  Notarial Acts.
- Must first hold the standard NJ notary commission above — no separate state track.
- Loan-signing training (e.g. NNA's Notary Signing Agent Training Course, ~4+ hours) is
  not state-required but strongly recommended; can lead to a private certification
  (e.g. American Society of Notaries "ASN Certified Notary Signing Agent").
- Signing services / national title companies commonly require their own background
  screening as vendor vetting before assigning loan-signing work, even though the state
  doesn't require it for the commission itself.

**Bottom line:** the regular commission is the cheap, fast prerequisite either way
(~$25 filing + a few hours of coursework, 5-year term). The Signing Agent path is the
same commission plus optional loan-signing training and possibly a private
certification/background check on top — there's no separate state license to chase.
